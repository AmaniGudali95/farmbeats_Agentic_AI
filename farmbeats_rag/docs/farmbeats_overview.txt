FarmBeats: Democratizing Data-Driven Agriculture Using Affordable Hardware
Published in IEEE Micro, January 2022, Vol 42(1): pp. 69-77
Authors: Ranveer Chandra, Manohar Swaminathan, Tusher Chakraborty, Jian Ding, Zerina Kapetanovic, Peeyush Kumar, Deepak Vasisht
Microsoft Research

=== ABSTRACT ===

The world needs to sustainably grow more food to feed the growing population of the planet. Data-driven agriculture is a promising technique that can help farmers be more productive, reduce costs, and enable adoption of sustainable agricultural practices. However, the adoption of data-driven agriculture is limited by a lack of affordable technologies, for broadband, sensing, imaging, and insights. In this article, we present an overview of Project FarmBeats, a research project that started in 2014 to increase the adoption of data-driven agricultural practices. We provide an overview of the various components of the FarmBeats architecture, and details of the hardware innovations.

=== INTRODUCTION ===

The agricultural sector feeds the world. It employs nearly 1 billion people globally and contributes to the GDP of most countries. However, there is an urgent need to increase agricultural productivity. The global population is projected to reach nearly 10 billion by 2050, and the demand for food is expected to increase by 70%. At the same time, arable land is shrinking due to urbanization and climate change, and water resources are becoming increasingly scarce. Data-driven agriculture is a promising approach to increasing productivity while reducing the environmental footprint of agriculture.

Data-driven agriculture involves the use of data from sensors, cameras, drones, satellites, and weather stations to make better decisions about crop management. For example, soil moisture sensors can be used to schedule irrigation more efficiently, reducing water usage by up to 30%. Drone-based imaging can be used to detect crop stress early, enabling farmers to take corrective action before yield losses occur. Weather forecasts can be used to optimize planting and harvesting schedules.

However, the adoption of data-driven agriculture is currently limited by the high cost of the required technologies. Existing soil sensor systems cost several thousand dollars per farm. Broadband internet connectivity is often unavailable or unaffordable in rural areas. Drone-based imaging requires expensive drones and skilled operators. And the data analytics and AI tools required to make sense of all this data are complex and expensive.

More than half of farmers in the United States need a second income to stay afloat. The situation is more dire for smallholder farmers. An average farmer in Sub-Saharan Africa earns less than $2 per day. For these farmers, the cost of existing data-driven agriculture technologies is simply prohibitive.

Project FarmBeats started in 2014 with the goal of making data-driven agriculture affordable and accessible to all farmers, regardless of their location or financial resources. The project has focused on innovations in four key areas: affordable broadband connectivity, affordable sensing, affordable imaging, and AI-driven insights.

=== FARMBEATS ARCHITECTURE ===

The FarmBeats system consists of four main components: FarmBeats Edge (deployed on the farm), FarmBeats Hub (a local gateway), FarmBeats Cloud (Azure-based backend), and FarmBeats Apps (farmer-facing applications).

FarmBeats Edge includes IoT sensors deployed across the farm to measure soil properties, weather conditions, and crop health. These sensors transmit data wirelessly to the FarmBeats Hub, which aggregates the data and sends it to the FarmBeats Cloud for storage and analysis.

The FarmBeats Hub runs on commodity hardware (a Raspberry Pi or similar device) and uses TV white spaces or other wireless technologies to connect to the internet. It also runs edge AI models to provide real-time insights without requiring a constant internet connection.

FarmBeats Cloud is built on Microsoft Azure and provides storage, processing, and AI capabilities. It includes time-series databases for sensor data, object stores for images, and machine learning pipelines for training and deploying AI models.

FarmBeats Apps include both web and mobile applications that present insights to farmers in a simple, actionable format. The apps provide recommendations for irrigation, fertilization, and pest control based on sensor data and AI models.

=== AFFORDABLE BROADBAND CONNECTIVITY ===

One of the biggest challenges for data-driven agriculture in rural areas is the lack of affordable broadband connectivity. Traditional cellular and fiber-optic networks are expensive to deploy in rural areas with low population density. As a result, many farms lack reliable internet access.

FarmBeats addresses this challenge by using TV white spaces (TVWS) for broadband connectivity. TV white spaces are unused portions of the TV spectrum that can be used for wireless broadband. These frequencies have excellent propagation characteristics, allowing them to travel long distances and penetrate obstacles like trees and buildings.

The FarmBeats TVWS system uses commodity hardware (a standard PC with a TVWS radio card) to create a wireless network that can cover an entire farm at a fraction of the cost of traditional broadband solutions. The system can provide speeds of up to 16 Mbps at ranges of up to 10 km, making it suitable for most farm data collection needs.

FarmBeats has deployed TVWS networks on farms in the US, India, and Africa, demonstrating the feasibility of this approach in diverse environments.

=== AFFORDABLE SENSING ===

Existing soil sensing technologies are expensive and require dense deployments to provide accurate, spatially resolved data. A typical soil moisture sensor costs $50-$200, and a farm may need dozens of sensors to cover all fields. This cost is prohibitive for most farmers.

FarmBeats has developed several approaches to reduce the cost of soil sensing. One approach is to use low-cost sensors (costing less than $10) that are less accurate but can be deployed densely across a farm. Machine learning models are then used to calibrate these low-cost sensors against a smaller number of high-quality reference sensors, improving their accuracy.

Another approach is to reduce the number of sensors required by using spatial interpolation algorithms to estimate soil properties at unmeasured locations based on measurements from nearby sensors. This allows farmers to get spatially resolved data with fewer sensors.

FarmBeats has also developed a novel approach to soil sensing using existing farm infrastructure. By analyzing the electrical characteristics of the soil around existing metal pipes and cables (such as irrigation pipes), FarmBeats can estimate soil moisture without deploying any additional sensors. This approach, called "pipe-based sensing," has been demonstrated on farms in the US.

The FarmBeats sensor system measures a range of soil properties including: soil moisture content (volumetric water content), soil temperature at multiple depths (5cm, 10cm, 20cm, 30cm), electrical conductivity (which correlates with soil salinity and nutrient content), and ambient temperature and humidity above the soil surface.

=== NDVI AND CROP HEALTH MONITORING ===

NDVI, or Normalized Difference Vegetation Index, is a measure of plant health calculated from remote sensing data. It is calculated as (NIR - Red) / (NIR + Red), where NIR is the near-infrared reflectance and Red is the red reflectance. NDVI values range from -1 to 1, with values above 0.5 indicating dense, healthy vegetation and values below 0.2 indicating sparse or stressed vegetation.

FarmBeats uses both drone-based and satellite-based imaging to calculate NDVI maps of farms. Drone-based imaging provides higher resolution (as fine as 1 cm per pixel) but covers smaller areas and requires manual operation. Satellite-based imaging covers large areas automatically but at lower resolution (10-30 meters per pixel) and is subject to cloud cover.

FarmBeats has developed algorithms to fuse drone and satellite imagery to get the best of both worlds: high resolution maps updated frequently. The system uses deep learning models to fill in gaps in satellite coverage due to clouds, using historical satellite data and drone images as inputs.

NDVI monitoring can detect crop stress early, often before it is visible to the naked eye. Common causes of crop stress that can be detected by NDVI monitoring include water stress (insufficient or excessive irrigation), nutrient deficiency (especially nitrogen deficiency), pest and disease damage, and frost damage.

=== IRRIGATION MANAGEMENT ===

Irrigation is the largest use of water in agriculture, accounting for about 70% of global freshwater withdrawals. Inefficient irrigation wastes water and can damage crops through over- or under-watering. FarmBeats aims to improve irrigation efficiency through real-time monitoring and AI-driven recommendations.

The FarmBeats irrigation management system uses soil moisture sensors to monitor the water content of the soil at multiple depths. When soil moisture falls below a threshold (which varies by crop and growth stage), the system recommends irrigation. The system also considers weather forecasts to avoid irrigating just before rain.

Field trials have shown that the FarmBeats irrigation management system can reduce water usage by 25-30% while maintaining or improving crop yields. The system has been deployed on potato farms in the US Pacific Northwest, where potato is a major crop and water resources are limited.

The recommended soil moisture thresholds for common crops are: corn/maize should be irrigated when soil moisture falls below 50% field capacity; wheat is more drought-tolerant and can be irrigated at 40% field capacity; potatoes require consistent moisture and should be irrigated at 60% field capacity; tomatoes need careful moisture management and should be kept between 50-70% field capacity.

=== AI AND MACHINE LEARNING ===

FarmBeats uses a range of AI and machine learning techniques to generate insights from sensor and imaging data. These include: time series forecasting models for predicting crop yield and soil properties; computer vision models for analyzing drone and satellite imagery; anomaly detection models for identifying unusual patterns in sensor data that may indicate crop stress or equipment failure; and recommendation systems for suggesting irrigation, fertilization, and pest control actions.

One of the key challenges in applying AI to agriculture is the lack of labeled training data. FarmBeats has addressed this challenge by developing techniques for data augmentation (artificially increasing the size of training datasets) and transfer learning (adapting models trained on one crop or region to another).

FarmBeats has also developed a novel approach to model deployment called "edge AI." Instead of sending all sensor data to the cloud for analysis, FarmBeats runs lightweight AI models directly on the FarmBeats Hub (a low-cost edge device). This reduces latency, bandwidth requirements, and cloud computing costs. Only the model outputs (insights and recommendations) are sent to the cloud.

=== CONNECTIVITY CHALLENGES ===

Rural connectivity remains a significant challenge for agricultural IoT deployments. The FarmBeats system has been designed to operate with intermittent connectivity. When the internet connection is unavailable, the FarmBeats Hub stores sensor data locally and syncs it to the cloud when connectivity is restored. Edge AI models running on the Hub continue to provide real-time insights even without internet access.

The system uses TV white space spectrum for primary connectivity, with cellular as a backup. TV white spaces operate in the 54-698 MHz frequency range and can provide coverage over distances of 5-10 km per base station, making them well-suited for agricultural deployments where farms may be spread over large areas.

=== FIELD DEPLOYMENTS AND RESULTS ===

FarmBeats has been deployed on multiple farms across the United States, India, and Africa. Key results from these deployments include:

Water savings: farms using the FarmBeats irrigation management system have reduced water usage by 25-30% compared to traditional irrigation scheduling methods.

Early detection of crop stress: NDVI monitoring has enabled farmers to detect crop stress an average of 7-10 days earlier than would be possible through visual inspection alone, allowing earlier intervention and reducing yield losses.

Yield improvement: farms using FarmBeats have reported yield improvements of 5-10% compared to control fields, attributed to better irrigation management and earlier detection and treatment of crop stress.

Cost savings: the affordable sensors and TVWS connectivity used by FarmBeats reduce the cost of precision agriculture by 10x compared to existing commercial solutions, making it accessible to smallholder farmers.

=== CONCLUSION ===

FarmBeats demonstrates that data-driven agriculture can be made affordable and accessible through innovations in connectivity, sensing, imaging, and AI. By reducing costs by an order of magnitude compared to existing solutions, FarmBeats opens the door to data-driven agriculture for the billions of smallholder farmers who currently cannot afford it.

Future work will focus on further reducing costs, improving the accuracy and reliability of the system, and expanding the range of crops and environments supported. The FarmBeats team is also working on open-sourcing the software components of the system to enable a broader ecosystem of innovation.

The FarmVibes project, a successor to FarmBeats, has open-sourced the AI and data processing components of the system on GitHub, enabling researchers and developers to build on this work. Key open-source components include FarmVibes.AI for data fusion and analysis, and FarmVibes.Connect for rural broadband connectivity.
